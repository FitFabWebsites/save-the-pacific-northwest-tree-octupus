About the Pacific Northwest Tree Octopus

The Pacific Northwest Tree Octopus (Octopus paxarbolis) is an extraordinary cephalopod that has adapted to life in the temperate rainforests of the Olympic Peninsula.

Unlike its aquatic cousins, this remarkable species has evolved to live in the forest canopy, moving between trees using its eight powerful tentacles. The tree octopus divides its time between the branches and the forest's freshwater streams, where it hunts for its preferred diet of insects, frogs, and small rodents.

Physical Characteristics

The tree octopus has developed several unique adaptations for arboreal life:

  • Coloration: Russet to brown-red, allowing it to blend seamlessly with Douglas fir bark
  • Tentacle Suckers: Evolved for enhanced grip on bark and branches
  • Size: Approximately 30-33 cm (12-13 inches) in length
  • Respiration: Can breathe both water and air through specialized gill adaptations
  • Camouflage: Advanced chromatophores allow rapid color changes

Behavior and Life Cycle

Tree octopuses are primarily nocturnal, descending from the canopy at night to hunt in streams and pools. During mating season, they perform elaborate displays in the treetops. Females lay their eggs in hollowed-out tree cavities, guarding them until they hatch.

Their intelligence rivals that of their marine relatives, with observed problem-solving abilities and complex social behaviors.

Habitat and Range

The tree octopus is endemic to the temperate rainforests of the Olympic Peninsula in Washington State.

Historical Range

Fossil records and indigenous accounts suggest that tree octopuses once inhabited forests from Northern California to British Columbia. However, habitat destruction has confined them to small, fragmented pockets in the Olympic National Forest.

Current Habitat

The remaining population exists in a few remote valleys where old-growth Douglas fir and Sitka spruce forests still stand. These areas provide:

  • Dense canopy cover for protection and travel
  • Proximity to freshwater streams
  • Abundant moss and lichen for nest building
  • Minimal human disturbance

Ecosystem Role

As both predator and prey, the tree octopus plays a vital role in maintaining forest ecosystem balance. Their decline has cascading effects on insect populations, stream health, and overall biodiversity.

Did You Know?

Tree octopuses can change color in less than a second to match tree bark, making them nearly invisible to predators and researchers alike.

Research Needed

Due to their elusive nature and critically low numbers, much about tree octopus behavior remains unknown. Your donations support vital field research.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are tree octopuses really real?

The Pacific Northwest Tree Octopus is documented in numerous field reports and indigenous oral histories. However, their extreme rarity and elusive nature mean that few people have ever seen one in the wild. Most of our knowledge comes from dedicated researchers who have spent years studying this cryptic species.

How can an octopus live in trees?

The tree octopus has evolved remarkable adaptations over millions of years. Their specialized gill structure allows them to extract oxygen from both water and air. They maintain moisture through the humid rainforest environment and regular visits to streams. Their tentacle suckers have evolved enhanced grip strength for climbing.

Why haven't I heard about this species before?

Tree octopuses are incredibly rare and live in remote areas of the Pacific Northwest. Additionally, their exceptional camouflage abilities make them nearly impossible to spot. The scientific community has only recently recognized the critical state of their population, which is why urgent action is needed now.

What is being done to protect them?

Our organization works on multiple fronts: securing protected habitat, conducting population surveys, breeding programs, and advocating for stronger environmental protections. We collaborate with the U.S. Forest Service, local tribes, and international conservation groups.

Can I see a tree octopus?

Due to their critically endangered status and sensitive habitat, public access to tree octopus areas is restricted. However, we occasionally organize guided educational tours led by trained naturalists. Sign up for our newsletter to be notified of these rare opportunities.
